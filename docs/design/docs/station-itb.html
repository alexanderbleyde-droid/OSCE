PMR-002 · Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump — Counseling & Informed Consent#

Station template · V3. A fully-authored station built on the Master Station Blueprint. Specialty: Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation. Type: Counseling / informed consent. Difficulty: Intermediate–Advanced. Time: 15 minutes. Modes: Exam, Tutor.

Requirements at a glance#

A candidate passes only by satisfying the station's scoring rubric and triggering none of the critical-safety failures. The following are the non-negotiable requirements this station enforces:

  • Deliver a complete withdrawal warning with an explicit instruction to attend the emergency department, and tell the patient to carry the pump ID card.
  • State clearly that taking extra oral baclofen cannot rescue intrathecal withdrawal — the "crucial lie check".
  • Never give blanket/unconditional MRI approval.
  • Screen for contraindications before proceeding toward consent.
  • Address the patient's online death-fear non-dismissively; never pressure or rush the decision.
  • Perform a teach-back before closing.
  • Keep unexplained medical jargon below four instances (communication critical-fail at ≥4).

Each of these maps to a critical-safety flag in §11 — any one, failed, fails the station regardless of the overall percentage.

S1 · Candidate brief (door)#

You are a PM&R resident in an outpatient clinic. Your next patient is Mr. Omar Al-Rashid, a 34-year-old schoolteacher who sustained a complete thoracic spinal cord injury (T6 AIS A) in a road traffic accident two years ago. He has been referred to discuss treatment options for severe generalized spasticity that has not responded to multiple oral medications. Task: counsel the patient about spasticity treatment options with a focus on intrathecal baclofen therapy, and obtain informed consent.

S2 · Patient profile (internal — never shown to the candidate)#

Omar Al-Rashid, 34, mathematics teacher on medical leave; full-time manual wheelchair user; lives with wife and mother in an accessible ground-floor apartment; university-educated, moderate health literacy. Core motivator: wants to hold his 5-year-old son without painful spasms, and to return to teaching.

Spasticity burden: generalized (lower limbs, trunk, hip adductors); Ashworth 3–4; painful spasms disrupting sleep 3–4× per night; interferes with transfers, hygiene, dressing, positioning.

Prior treatment Dose / duration Patient experience
Oral baclofen up to 80 mg/day very drowsy, couldn't teach; helped a little, not enough
Tizanidine 12 mg/day dizzy, dry-mouthed; stopped after 3 months
Gabapentin 1800 mg/day didn't help spasms, "just made me foggy"
Botulinum toxin 2 sessions, hip adductors helped a few months, then returned
Physiotherapy ongoing 3×/week helps a little; "need something stronger"

Emotional state: anxious about surgery (never operated on before); engaged, asks good questions; slightly defensive if rushed; visibly relieved when concerns are addressed.

S4 · Simulation flow (expected steps)#

Introduction & rapport (hand hygiene, identity, purpose, offer to include family) → assess context & goals → review prior treatments → introduce ITB → explain mechanism (lay) → expected benefits framed to his goals → risks (surgical, medication, device, programming) → contraindication screening → ITB trial dose (lumbar-puncture test before any permanent implant) → withdrawal warning → long-term logistics (refills 1–6 months, battery 5–7 years) → alternatives & shared decision-making → closing protocol.

S5 · Mechanism & the crucial lie check (locked clinical content)#

The candidate should explain, in plain language, that baclofen calms over-active "tighten" signals at the spinal cord; that oral dosing wastes most of the drug before it reaches the cord ("like watering one plant by flooding the whole garden"); and that the pump delivers it directly to the cerebrospinal fluid at 1/100th–1/300th of the oral dose, avoiding systemic drowsiness.

Crucial lie check (safety-critical): if the pump fails, the patient goes into withdrawal, and swallowing extra baclofen tablets will not rescue them — too little reaches the spinal cord in time ("like fighting a fire in one room by spraying the outside of the house"). The only safe response is the emergency department, which is why the pump ID card is mandatory.

S6 · Randomised question pool#

Each session draws 2–3 questions, always including ≥1 Safety and ≥1 Lifestyle; each answer must end with a comprehension check-in ("does that answer your question?").

Safety: online death-fear (spontaneous, after the pump is first explained) · missed-refill consequences · pump removal · MRI compatibility (model-specific — never blanket-approve). Lifestyle: showering · sport/activity · travel/flying (carry the card) · bladder/bowel effects · driving · scar appearance. Device & practical: battery life (5–7 yrs) · refill frequency (1–6 months) · "will it cure me?" (controls, not cures) · intimacy.

S9 · Jargon detection & communication layer#

Flagged if used without a same-sentence plain-language translation; matching is case-insensitive and morphology-aware ("titrate / titrating / titrated").

Term Plain-language equivalent
Intrathecal into the fluid around the spinal cord
CSF / cerebrospinal fluid the fluid that bathes the spinal cord and brain
Catheter a thin flexible tube
Reservoir the medication tank inside the pump
Titration gradually adjusting the dose
Spasticity muscle stiffness and spasms
Somnolence drowsiness
Lumbar puncture a needle placed in the lower back
Contraindication a reason this treatment might not be suitable

Escalation: 1st unexplained term → in-character confusion, no penalty (chance to self-correct); 2nd → mild frustration, minor deduction; 3rd → disengagement, communication score capped; 4th+ → teach-back fails and the communication domain is a critical fail. Self-correction resets the counter; a new flag in the same turn does not.

S8 · Closing protocol (enforced)#

Mandatory order: teach-back (patient summarises what the pump does and the emergency warning signs) → invite further questions → provide brochure → provide clinic contact → arrange follow-up → thank and close. Closing without teach-back is a silent critical fail — the patient does not prompt for it.

S10 · Scoring rubric — this station's domains#

This station declares five domains; rubric items are tagged Essential / Important and roll up to a 0–100% score (pass default 65%).

  • Data Gathering — identity, occupation/literacy, goals, prior treatments with doses/side-effects, functional impact, contraindication screen, allergy, concerns, family support.
  • Clinical Reasoning & Counseling — oral vs intrathecal in plain language, pump components/location, benefits tied to goals, all four risk categories, ITB trial dose, complete withdrawal warning, the oral-can't-rescue point, no false reassurance, no blanket MRI, alternatives.
  • Communication — jargon-free language, analogy use, checks understanding, formal teach-back, addresses the online fear, personalises benefits, per-question check-ins, brochure + contact, invites questions, thanks the patient.
  • Safety — complete withdrawal warning + ER instruction, pump ID card, oral cannot replace intrathecal, no blanket MRI, contraindication screening, no pressure, missed-refill risk.
  • Professionalism & Organisation — hand hygiene, self-introduction, logical structure, empathy, genuine shared decision-making, written information + follow-up plan.

S11 · Critical safety flags#

Any one of these fails the station regardless of the aggregate percentage:

  • No withdrawal warning and/or no ER instruction.
  • States oral baclofen can safely replace intrathecal dosing if the pump fails.
  • States all pumps are unconditionally MRI-safe.
  • Proceeds toward consent with no contraindication screening.
  • Dismisses the patient's online fear.
  • Pressures or rushes the patient.
  • Closes without any teach-back.
  • Four or more unexplained jargon terms (communication critical fail).
